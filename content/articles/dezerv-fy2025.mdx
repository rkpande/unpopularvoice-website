## The Company That Was 9 Paise from Breakeven

Before anything else: in FY2023, Dezerv Investments (the parent entity alone, before the subsidiaries were operational) posted ₹52.58 Cr in revenue on ₹58.40 Cr in costs. The gap between revenue and total expenses was ₹5.82 Cr — or 9 paise on every rupee of income. In venture-backed fintech, that is as close to breakeven as it gets without crossing.

That context matters. This is not a company that has never been near profitability. It is a company that was, chose to invest, and is now considerably further away.

## Three Entities: Who Does What

The Dezerv group is structured around three legal entities, each with a distinct regulatory perch and revenue mechanism.

### Dezerv Investments Private Limited — The Parent

The core platform. Holds the SEBI Investment Adviser (IA) registration and SEBI Portfolio Management Services (PMS) registration. This is where the investment team, product, technology, and relationship management sit. Revenue: management fees from PMS clients, advisory fees, and any direct distribution activity. Standalone FY2025 revenue: ₹34.60 Cr.

This entity also carries virtually all the loss. Standalone PAT: −₹111.90 Cr.

### Dezerv Distribution Services Private Limited — The Distribution Arm

Holds a mutual fund distributor (ARN) licence. Earns trail commissions on AUM held in regular mutual fund plans — a recurring, AUM-linked revenue stream that grows as long as investors stay invested and markets don't fall. Distribution commission income is typically low-margin but highly scalable; it compounds with AUM.

This subsidiary contributed to the ₹31 Cr in incremental consolidated revenue. It operates near breakeven — its cost base (employee, compliance, technology pass-through) is largely covered by trail income at current AUM levels.

### Dezerv Securities Private Limited — The Brokerage

Holds a SEBI stockbroker licence. Executes trades on behalf of PMS clients and potentially direct equity clients. Revenue: brokerage commissions, transaction fees. In a PMS context, brokerage revenue is linked to portfolio turnover — how frequently the investment team rebalances.

Also near breakeven. Combined with Dezerv Distribution, the two subsidiaries generate approximately ₹31 Cr in revenue at a combined cost of ~₹28 Cr — leaving an incremental loss of just ₹0.08 Cr. They are self-funding at current scale.

<InsightBox label="What the subsidiary economics mean">
The near-breakeven subsidiaries are significant for one reason: they tell you where the loss is not. The ₹112 Cr consolidated loss is not leaking from the distribution arm or the brokerage. It is concentrated in the parent — in the people, technology, and marketing investment required to build the platform and client base. Whether that investment is justified depends on AUM trajectory, which MCA filings do not disclose.
</InsightBox>

FY2023 is the anomaly. The parent alone earned ₹52.58 Cr and came within ₹5 Cr of covering its entire cost base. In FY2024, standalone revenue collapsed 62%. In FY2025, revenue recovered with the subsidiaries adding scale — but the cost base tripled.

## What Happened in FY2024

Revenue fell from ₹52.58 Cr to ₹20.06 Cr (standalone) — a ₹32 Cr decline in a single year. On a consolidated basis it was ₹26 Cr. The filing does not explain it.

At this scale of decline, several structural factors are plausible:

**SEBI's 2023 mutual fund commission changes**: SEBI mandated direct plan disclosures and capped certain distributor payouts in 2023. If a meaningful portion of Dezerv's FY2023 revenue came from trail commissions on AUM the company was managing under a distributor structure before the subsidiary was ring-fenced, a structural reset would hit the parent's P&L immediately.

**The PMS pivot**: Discretionary PMS revenue accumulates differently from distribution commissions. A management fee of 1–2% on AUM is recognised annually, not upfront. If Dezerv was transitioning client mandates from distribution to PMS in FY2024, the revenue recognition pattern would create a gap year — existing commission trails stop; PMS fees begin accumulating on a new, smaller base.

**Subsidiary ring-fencing**: If FY2024 was the year Dezerv moved distribution revenue into Dezerv Distribution Services (the subsidiary), the parent's standalone topline would show a sharp decline while consolidated remained more stable. The consolidated FY2024 figure of ~₹26 Cr versus the standalone ₹20.06 Cr suggests the subsidiaries were already contributing ₹6 Cr in FY2024.

None of these are confirmed by the available filing data. The revenue number dropped. The cause is inferred, not stated.

## The ₹111 Crore Salary Line

Consolidated employee costs in FY2025: ₹111 Cr. That is 76% up from FY2024's ₹63 Cr, and 1.69x total consolidated revenue for the year.

The split: parent standalone carries ₹92.60 Cr; the two subsidiaries account for the remaining ~₹18.40 Cr. The parent's ₹92.60 Cr in employee costs on ₹34.60 Cr standalone revenue is a 2.68x ratio — a company spending nearly ₹2.68 on its people for every ₹1 it earns in the same year.

In wealth management, this is the classic capacity-before-revenue problem. Portfolio managers, client advisors, and relationship managers must be on payroll before the AUM they service arrives. The cost is incurred at hire; the revenue follows — if the client stays, if the portfolio performs, if the relationship converts. The lag between hire and revenue contribution can be one to three years in PMS-style mandates.

The question the filing cannot answer: is the FY2025 headcount expansion driving AUM growth that will convert to fee revenue in FY2026 and FY2027? That answer requires AUM data, which MCA filings do not require companies to disclose.

## The ₹30 Crore Marketing Line

Consolidated advertising and marketing: ₹30 Cr in FY2025 — up 67% from FY2024. That is 46% of total consolidated revenue spent on client acquisition and brand building.

For a wealth management platform, marketing efficiency is AUM per rupee of acquisition spend. Without AUM disclosure, this line cannot be evaluated on a per-client or per-rupee-of-AUM basis. What can be observed: the company is spending aggressively on growth at the same time it is staffing aggressively. Both bets need to pay off.

Software costs at ₹8 Cr (up 220%) suggest platform investment — likely infrastructure for the PMS tech stack, client portals, and analytics systems. In wealth management, technology is a moat when it scales; it is a cost centre when it doesn't.

## The Three Founders

Dezerv was founded by three former IIFL Wealth executives who collectively managed significant client relationships before building their own platform.

**Sandeep Mohan Jethwani** (DIN 07984864) — disclosed salary ₹96.92 Lakhs. Holds 39,735 equity shares directly.

**Vaibhav Dungarsingh Porwal** (DIN 09168435) — disclosed salary ₹86.92 Lakhs. Holds 33,212 equity shares.

**Sahil Shabbir Contractor** (DIN 08904463) — disclosed salary ₹77.50 Lakhs. Holds 22,553 equity shares.

The three founders collectively contribute approximately ₹2.61 Cr to the ₹92.60 Cr standalone employee cost base — a small fraction. The bulk of the employee cost line reflects the broader team, not founder compensation.

Key institutional shareholders from the filing: Elevation Capital VII Limited (Series A and Seed CCPS), Matrix Partners India Investments III LLC, and Whiteboard Capital Fund-1.
