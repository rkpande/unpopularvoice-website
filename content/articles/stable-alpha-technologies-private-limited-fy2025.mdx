## Rs 104 Cr Revenue. Rs 3.6 Cr Real Business. The Gap Is the Story.

Stable Money reported Rs 104.4 Cr in consolidated revenue in FY2025 — an 82x jump from the year before. But the standalone entity, Stable-Alpha Technologies itself, earned just Rs 3.6 Cr from operations. The other Rs 100.8 Cr appears to flow through a subsidiary, likely representing bond trade throughput rather than fee income the company actually keeps.

Stable Money is a Bengaluru-based fintech platform that helps retail investors buy fixed deposits and bonds. It earns money by distributing these products — taking a commission or spread when users invest through its platform.

The company tells a growth story. The filing tells a different one: a business burning Rs 45 Cr a year to generate Rs 3.6 Cr in core standalone revenue, with the giant topline number doing the heavy lifting in pitch decks while the losses remain entirely real.

On the surface, this looks like a rocketship: revenue from Rs 1.3 Cr to Rs 104.4 Cr in a single year. Total assets nearly quadrupled. Cash reserves tripled. The auditor issued an unqualified opinion — no red flags in the accounting itself.

But look at the loss line. It didn't just grow — it tripled, from Rs 12.8 Cr to Rs 44.9 Cr. Revenue grew 80x; losses grew 3.5x. If revenue were real fee income, the loss should be narrowing, not widening. That disconnect is the first signal that most of the topline is pass-through.

## What the Numbers Actually Mean

The standalone filing strips the illusion away. Stable-Alpha Technologies — the parent company — reported just Rs 3.6 Cr in revenue from operations and Rs 6.5 Cr in other income (likely interest on its cash pile and inter-company items). Meanwhile, the consolidated entity booked Rs 104.4 Cr in revenue from operations.

That Rs 100.8 Cr gap is flowing through a subsidiary. In bond distribution businesses, the gross transaction value — the face value of bonds bought and sold — often gets recorded as revenue in the subsidiary's books. But the actual income the business retains is a thin commission, typically 0.5–2% of the transaction value. The filing suggests the consolidated topline is dominated by this throughput, not by retained fees.

The consolidated balance sheet shows Rs 16.4 Cr in inventories (goods held for sale or consumption). For a fintech platform that distributes bonds, inventory likely means unsold bonds sitting on the subsidiary's books. This suggests the company may be warehousing securities on its own balance sheet — taking principal risk, not just earning commissions. That is a fundamentally different business model than what "India's fixed income platform" implies.

<InsightBox type="flag" number="01" title="Rs 13.90 spent for every Rs 1 earned from core operations">
  On a standalone basis, Stable-Alpha earned Rs 3.6 Cr from operations and spent Rs 49.8 Cr running the business. That is Rs 13.90 out the door for every rupee of operating revenue. Even using the consolidated topline, total expenses of Rs 156.9 Cr against Rs 104.4 Cr revenue means Rs 1.50 spent for every rupee reported — and that is before you strip out the throughput illusion. The company is not subsidizing growth. It is subsidizing existence.
</InsightBox>

<InsightBox type="flag" number="02" title="Employee costs doubled. Other expenses quintupled. Revenue didn't keep up.">
  Employee benefit expenses (salaries, ESOP costs, benefits) jumped from Rs 8.7 Cr to Rs 21.5 Cr on a consolidated basis — a 2.5x increase. But "other expenses" (which typically includes marketing, technology, office costs, and customer acquisition spending) exploded from Rs 7.4 Cr to Rs 33.5 Cr — a 4.5x surge. That Rs 33.5 Cr in other expenses alone is 9.4x the standalone operating revenue. This pattern is consistent with a company spending aggressively on customer acquisition to inflate platform usage numbers, which then show up as impressive-looking throughput in the consolidated topline.
</InsightBox>

<InsightBox type="warning" number="03" title="Rs 16.4 Cr in inventory on a platform that's supposed to be a marketplace">
  A pure distribution platform shouldn't carry inventory. The Rs 16.4 Cr in inventories on the consolidated balance sheet suggests the subsidiary is buying bonds and holding them before selling to customers — or failing to sell them entirely. Either way, this introduces principal risk: if bond prices fall or buyers don't materialize, the company eats the loss. For a two-year-old startup, warehousing Rs 16.4 Cr in securities is a bold bet that most users probably don't realize they're indirectly underwriting.
</InsightBox>

## Why This Moment Matters

The cash flow statement tells the timing story. In FY2025, Stable Money raised Rs 123.6 Cr through financing activities (cash from financing — money raised from investors or borrowings), against operating cash outflows of Rs 47 Cr and investing outflows of Rs 77.1 Cr. The company entered FY2025 with roughly Rs 19 Cr in cash and ended with Rs 55.1 Cr — but only because fresh capital arrived.

Public reports indicate that Fundamentum Partnership led a funding round, with participation from Aditya Birla Ventures, adding to earlier backing from Lightspeed India and Matrix Partners India. The Rs 123.6 Cr in financing inflows aligns with this round. With the company burning approximately Rs 47 Cr annually on operations alone, the fundraise wasn't a growth accelerant — it was a survival mechanism. At the current burn rate, the Rs 55 Cr cash balance buys roughly 12-14 months of runway, assuming no further deterioration.

The loss trajectory makes this timeline tighter than it appears. Losses have scaled from Rs 2.1 Cr (in a partial year) to Rs 12.8 Cr to Rs 44.9 Cr. If the trend line continues into FY2026 — and the company's own hiring signals suggest it plans to keep scaling aggressively — the quarterly burn could comfortably exceed Rs 15 Cr, shrinking that runway to under a year.

Meanwhile, the company's blog publishes educational content about IDCW vs. growth mutual fund options and fixed deposit comparisons — a user acquisition play that makes sense only if the underlying unit economics can eventually support the spend. Right now, they cannot.

## Who Holds the Keys

Founders Saurabh Subhash Jain and Mallu Harish Reddy hold 60,000 shares each — the largest individual blocks. Institutional investors dominate the cap table: Lightspeed India Partners III holds approximately 48,425 shares across two tranches, Matrix Partners India IV holds roughly 47,433 shares across multiple vehicles, and RTP Capital Limited holds 33,877 shares. Notable individual shareholders include Kunal Bahl and Rohit Kumar Bansal — the Snapdeal co-founders — with 990 shares each. Dezerv Innovation Fund and AL Trust round out the investor roster. The two founders appear to retain meaningful but not overwhelming control. With Lightspeed, Matrix, and RTP collectively holding significant stakes, the next fundraise will likely involve another round of dilution — and the terms of that round will reveal whether the market still buys the topline narrative.

<InsightBox type="insight" number="04" title="Blue-chip investor roster doesn't change the unit economics">
  Lightspeed, Matrix Partners, Fundamentum, and Aditya Birla Ventures are credible names. Their presence signals conviction in the market opportunity for retail fixed-income distribution. But conviction and profitability are different things. These investors backed the company when losses were Rs 12.8 Cr. Losses are now Rs 44.9 Cr. The next funding conversation will be harder — not because the opportunity has shrunk, but because the cost of proving it has exploded.
</InsightBox>
