## Rs 104 Crore Revenue From a Company That Made Rs 1.3 Crore Last Year

An 82x jump in consolidated revenue in a single year. That's the headline number from Stable Money's FY25 filing — from Rs 1.3 Cr to Rs 104.4 Cr. But before anyone declares this the fastest-growing fintech in India, look one line down: the company spent Rs 157 Cr to earn that Rs 104 Cr. For every rupee of revenue, Rs 1.40 walked out the door.

Stable Money — operated by Stable-Alpha Technologies Private Limited — is a Bengaluru-based fintech that helps retail investors buy fixed deposits, bonds, and other fixed-income instruments. Think of it as a marketplace where you compare FD rates across banks and NBFCs, then invest through the app. The company earns commission and distribution fees.

The world sees an exploding revenue line. The filing shows something more complicated: a business that may be buying and holding bonds on its own books (Rs 164 Cr of inventory that didn't exist a year ago), burning cash at an accelerating rate, and racing to raise capital to keep the engine running.

**The revenue story** looks staggering: Rs 104.4 Cr in operating revenue where there was effectively zero two years ago. But what's driving it? On a standalone basis — the parent company alone — revenue from operations is just Rs 3.6 Cr. The other Rs 100+ Cr flows through a subsidiary. This suggests the subsidiary is the actual revenue-generating entity, likely holding the regulatory licenses needed to distribute or trade fixed-income products.

**The cost story** is where things get real. Employee costs jumped from Rs 8.7 Cr to Rs 21.5 Cr — a 2.5x increase that reflects aggressive hiring. Other expenses ballooned to Rs 33.5 Cr, up 4.5x, likely driven by customer acquisition and marketing. Add it all up: Rs 157 Cr in total expenses, 28x higher than the prior year's Rs 5.6 Cr operating cost base (FY2023 annualized).

**Cash flow tells the sharpest story.** On a standalone basis (where cash flow data is available), operations consumed Rs 47 Cr in cash (CFO — cash flow from operations, meaning the cash the day-to-day business actually burned). Investing activities consumed another Rs 77.1 Cr — likely capital deployed into bond inventory or fixed deposits held for sale. The company raised Rs 123.6 Cr from financing activities (CFF — cash from fundraising and borrowings), almost entirely equity. Net result: cash on hand at Rs 46.2 Cr on a standalone basis, Rs 55.1 Cr on a consolidated basis.

**The balance sheet** reveals a hidden structural shift. Inventory — which was Rs 0.5 Cr in FY24 — is now Rs 16.4 Cr on a consolidated basis. For a distribution platform, "inventory" almost certainly means fixed-income securities the company has purchased and is holding on its own books before selling to customers. This isn't just a marketplace anymore; the company is taking principal risk.

Total equity stands at Rs 112.3 Cr consolidated. Essentially zero debt. The balance sheet is funded almost entirely by venture capital — clean, but depleting fast.

## What the Filing Actually Shows

<InsightBox type="flag" number="01" title="Standalone revenue is Rs 3.6 Cr — 97% of revenue comes from a subsidiary">
  The parent entity, Stable-Alpha Technologies, booked just Rs 3.6 Cr in operating revenue versus Rs 49.8 Cr in expenses during FY25, making it a net cost center. On a consolidated basis, the subsidiary generated Rs 104.4 Cr in revenue. This means all the revenue action — and the associated regulatory risk — sits in a subsidiary entity. Anyone evaluating this company should understand which legal entity holds the distribution licenses, the customer relationships, and the inventory.
</InsightBox>

<InsightBox type="warning" number="02" title="Rs 164 Cr in consolidated inventory signals a business model shift">
  A pure-play FD comparison platform shouldn't have Rs 16.4 Cr in inventory on a consolidated basis. (The filing doesn't break down inventory composition, but in a fintech distributing bonds and FDs, this almost certainly represents securities purchased and held for resale.) This is a material change from the asset-light model of simply connecting buyers with banks. It introduces credit risk, interest-rate risk, and the need for significantly more working capital. The filing does not disclose the nature of this inventory in the data provided.
</InsightBox>

<InsightBox type="positive" number="03" title="Net profit margin improved from -999% to -43% — directionally right">
  In FY24, consolidated net profit margin (net loss divided by revenue) was an eye-watering negative 999%. In FY25, it improved to negative 43%. That's still a loss, but the denominator grew so fast that each rupee of revenue now loses Rs 0.43 instead of Rs 10. If unit economics hold at scale, this trajectory points toward a plausible — if distant — path to breakeven. The key question is whether the Rs 104 Cr revenue figure includes principal trading gains that may not recur.
</InsightBox>

## Why This Matters Now

Stable Money raised a round led by Fundamentum Partnership with participation from Aditya Birla Ventures, according to publicly available reports. The FY25 financing cash flow of Rs 123.6 Cr (standalone) aligns with this raise. Existing backers Lightspeed India and Matrix Partners India (Z47) also appear on the cap table from earlier rounds.

The timing makes sense when you look at the numbers: the company burned Rs 47 Cr in operating cash in FY25 on a standalone basis, with an additional Rs 77 Cr deployed into investments. At the FY24-end cash position of just Rs 8.1 Cr (standalone), the company would have run dry within weeks. The Fundamentum-led round wasn't an acceleration bet — it was oxygen. The Rs 46.2 Cr in standalone cash at year-end, against quarterly burn rates approaching Rs 12 Cr, gives the company roughly 4 months of runway at the current pace before it needs to either dramatically cut costs or raise again.

The 82x revenue jump also coincides with an aggressive push into bond distribution and possibly principal trading — which explains both the revenue explosion and the inventory buildup. This is a classic fintech scaling playbook: shift from pure marketplace to taking inventory positions, which inflates revenue but also inflates risk. Whether regulators view this favorably — and whether unit economics hold when rates cycle — remains the open question for the next 12 months.

Notably, Kunal Bahl and Rohit Bansal — co-founders of Snapdeal and partners at Titan Capital — hold 990 shares each, a small but symbolic angel position. Dezerv Innovation Fund and AL Trust round out the cap table with smaller stakes.
